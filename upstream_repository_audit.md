# Upstream Repository Audit

Audit date: 2026-05-29

Repositories inspected:

- `K-Dense-AI/scientific-agent-skills`, cloned to `/tmp/ling-bot-upstream-audit/scientific-agent-skills`

This audit focuses on reusable capability patterns for a general AI/ML/RL research skill. It intentionally does not treat the existence of a skill as proof of usefulness.

## Executive Assessment

`K-Dense-AI/scientific-agent-skills` is a broad scientific skill library. Its strongest reusable pieces are general research-method workflows: literature review, paper lookup, citation management, hypothesis generation, scientific critical thinking, peer review, statistical analysis, and writing. Its weaknesses for AI/ML/RL are domain mismatch, overbroad mandates such as forced figure generation, biomedical bias in some frameworks, and inconsistent enforcement of evidence traceability.

`Orchestra-Research/AI-Research-SKILLs` is much closer to AI research engineering. Its strongest pieces are the `autoresearch` orchestration layer, AI engineering tool skills, ML paper writing citation safeguards, evaluation harness skills, mechanistic interpretability skills, post-training/RL skills, experiment tracking skills, and Agent-Native Research Artifact (ARA) provenance and rigor-review patterns. Its weaknesses are tool/framework granularity, LLM-centric coverage, limited general literature-review depth, limited benchmark-design critique beyond specific harness invocation, and an autonomy posture that is too aggressive for a reliability-first research assistant.

The best upstream material is not a ready-made skill. It is a set of patterns: staged research state, evidence ledger, citation verification, experiment protocols locked before results, provenance tags, method-comparison matrices, reproducibility checklists, and severity-ranked rigor review.

## Repository Architecture

### K-Dense-AI/scientific-agent-skills

- Defines skills as directories under `skills/{skill-name}/` with `SKILL.md`, optional `references/`, `scripts/`, `assets/`, and templates.
- Covers 142 scientific skills across databases, packages, workflows, writing, and communication.
- Granularity varies widely: some skills are concrete package guides (`scikit-learn`, `stable-baselines3`), some are workflows (`literature-review`, `hypothesis-generation`), and some are integrations (`paperzilla`, `open-notebook`).
- Many skills depend on external APIs, CLIs, or scripts. Examples: `parallel-cli`, Exa, OpenRouter, BGPT MCP, Paperzilla CLI, Zotero API, Google Scholar scraping, PubMed APIs, LaTeX, LibreOffice.
- Strong progressive disclosure pattern: `SKILL.md` gives overview and workflow; `references/` contains more detail; `scripts/` provide reusable operations.
- Major risk: too much content is science-general or biomedical/clinical. Direct reuse would pollute an AI/ML/RL research skill with irrelevant priors and weakly relevant checklists.

### Orchestra-Research/AI-Research-SKILLs

- Defines skills as directories grouped by numbered AI-research categories, each with `SKILL.md` and usually `references/`; fewer bundled scripts.
- Also includes `.claude-plugin/marketplace.json` to package skills into category plugins.
- Granularity is mostly framework/tool-level: `vllm`, `trl-fine-tuning`, `lm-evaluation-harness`, `transformer-lens`, `wandb`, etc.
- Has one central orchestration skill: `0-autoresearch-skill`, plus ideation, writing, and ARA project-memory/rigor-review skills.
- Stronger AI-research fit than K-Dense, but often assumes the user is running experiments and writing code immediately.
- Major risk: an agent could over-focus on execution and leaderboard optimization before the research question, evidence base, and evaluation protocol are mature.

## Capabilities Absent or Weak in Both

- A first-class structured AI/ML/RL paper-reading template that separates problem, assumptions, method, claims, evidence, baselines, datasets, metrics, limitations, and reproducibility.
- Literature coverage auditing: query log, source mix, venue coverage, recency/classic balance, citation-chaining stopping criteria.
- Version-aware citation management for AI papers: arXiv preprint vs conference version, withdrawn/retracted papers, code/data artifact links.
- Explicit benchmark validity checks: contamination risk, train/test leakage, saturated benchmark warning, metric gaming, benchmark mismatch with claim.
- Baseline adequacy rubric for AI/ML/RL: strongest prior, simple baseline, compute-matched baseline, architecture-matched baseline, oracle/upper bound where relevant.
- Ablation design rubric: component isolation, interaction effects, hyperparameter fairness, negative controls, randomization, sensitivity analysis.
- RL-specific rigor beyond framework usage: environment versioning, seeds, stochasticity, return distributions, evaluation episodes, exploration budget, reward hacking, offline RL dataset quality, sim-to-real gaps.
- Reproducibility grading: code availability, dependency pins, hardware, seeds, configs, data splits, preprocessing, checkpoint availability, cost estimate.
- Claim calibration workflow: supported vs weakly supported vs speculative vs contradicted.
- Literature-to-hypothesis traceability: each hypothesis should cite gaps/evidence and declare falsification criteria.
- Handling of non-experimental AI research: theory, surveys, interpretability, systems, datasets, benchmarks, position papers.
- Practical “ask/stop/escalate” criteria for an assistant that is not supposed to autonomously run research indefinitely.
